\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================

Assessing and benchmarking the quality of subnational governance is a
fundamental challenge in development economics. Effective, transparent, and
accountable local administrations are widely recognised as prerequisites for
sustainable economic growth, equitable public service delivery, and citizen
welfare \citep{Kaufmann2010,Acemoglu2005}. Yet translating this normative
consensus into credible, reproducible, and policy-actionable scores remains
methodologically demanding: governance is inherently multidimensional, its
constituent indicators are correlated and heteroscedastic, inter-provincial
comparisons require objective weight derivation rather than expert opinion,
and retrospective dashboards alone cannot support prospective resource
allocation.

These challenges are acute in Vietnam, where three decades of \textit{Đổi
Mới}-era decentralisation have devolved an expanding portfolio of service
delivery, infrastructure investment, and regulatory responsibilities to the
63 provincial administrations \citep{Malesky2014,Nguyen2020}. Uniform
national policies interact with pronounced regional heterogeneity in
geography, demography, and fiscal capacity, producing divergent governance
outcomes across provinces that are difficult to monitor and compare.
Identifying high-performing and underperforming jurisdictions in a
methodologically rigorous manner is therefore a precondition for evidence-
based intergovernmental transfers, performance incentives, and targeted
capacity-building programmes.

The Provincial Governance and Public Administration Performance Index
(\PAPI) has been the principal instrument for this purpose since its
nationwide inception in 2011. Jointly developed by the Centre for Community
Support and Development Studies (CECODES), the Vietnam Fatherland Front
(VFF-CRT), and the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), \PAPI is an
annual citizen survey covering approximately 14{,}000 respondents
across all 63 provinces \citep{PAPI2024}. It quantifies provincial governance
quality across eight main criteria---civic participation, transparency,
vertical accountability, control of corruption, public administrative
procedures, public service delivery, environmental governance, and
e-governance---through 29 sub-criteria. By 2024, the index provides 14
consecutive annual cross-sections of panel data (2011--2024), constituting
882 province-year observations across a rich set of governance indicators.

\subsection*{Limitations of existing composite indexing approaches}

Despite its breadth and longevity, the analytical use of \PAPI data faces
several methodological limitations in the literature. First, composite
indices reported in the official \PAPI documentation employ equal weighting
of sub-criteria within each main criterion and equal weighting of criteria
at the aggregate level \citep{PAPI2024}. Equal weighting is transparent and
easily communicated, but it conflates criteria with very different
contributions to inter-provincial differentiation: a criterion dominated by
high-variance, low-correlated sub-indicators carries the same weight as one
where all provinces score similarly and sub-indicators are highly correlated.
Objective, data-driven weight determination that rewards discriminatory power
and penalises redundancy has not been applied to \PAPI in the published
literature.

Second, composite governance scores derived from a single Multi-Criteria
Decision Making (\MCDM) method are sensitive to methodological choice
\citep{Greco2019}. TOPSIS, VIKOR, PROMETHEE, COPRAS, EDAS, and SAW can
produce different rank orderings for the same data because they operationalise
``best alternative'' through different geometric and philosophical lenses.
Using a single method without sensitivity analysis or consensus aggregation
yields rankings that may lack robustness across reasonable methodological
alternatives.

Third, existing provincial governance analyses are inherently backward-
looking. They rank provinces on historical performance but cannot project how
current trajectories will translate into future scores. In a policy environment
where budget allocations and reform programmes must be decided one to two
years in advance, the ability to forecast provincial \PAPI scores for the
upcoming year would substantially enhance the decision-support value of the
index. To the best of our knowledge, no published study has applied machine
learning forecasting to \PAPI data.

Fourth, the \PAPI dataset exhibits substantial and structurally heterogeneous
missing data (\SI{13.4}{\percent} of cells; \citealp{PAPI2024-data}). The
environmental governance and e-governance dimensions were not measured in
early survey waves (2011--2017), and administrative disruptions have produced
entire province-year rows of missing data. Deterministic imputation or
complete-case analysis discards useful information and can introduce
systematic bias into downstream weighting and ranking.

\subsection*{Research gaps and motivation}

These observations motivate a unified analytical framework that addresses
all four limitations simultaneously. Three specific research gaps guide the
design of the proposed methodology:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{G\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.5em]

  \item \textbf{Objective, reproducible weighting.} No study has applied
    a variance-and-conflict-based objective weighting method to the full
    panel of \PAPI sub-criteria. The \CRITIC method (CRiteria Importance
    Through Intercriteria Correlation), which rewards criteria with high
    standard deviation and low pairwise correlation---\ie high discriminatory
    power and independence---provides a principled, fully deterministic
    alternative \citep{Diakoulaki1995}.

  \item \textbf{Consensus multi-method ranking.} A single \MCDM method cannot
    claim methodological supremacy over equally defensible alternatives.
    Fusing the outputs of multiple methods through Evidential Reasoning
    \citep{Yang2002}, which models inter-method disagreement explicitly as
    residual uncertainty, yields composite rankings that are robust to
    individual methodological assumptions and quantifies agreement via
    Kendall's concordance coefficient $W$.

  \item \textbf{Prospective governance forecasting.} No framework currently
    converts historical \PAPI trajectories into statistically calibrated
    one-year-ahead forecasts with associated prediction intervals. Filling
    this gap requires a machine learning pipeline capable of handling the
    specific constraints of \PAPI: a small balanced panel ($N = 63$
    entities, $T = 14$ observations per entity), structurally patterned
    missing data, multi-output prediction ($K = 8$ criteria jointly), and
    mandatory distribution-free uncertainty quantification to support
    risk-aware policy decisions.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Contributions}

This paper makes four principal contributions to the literature
on MCDM-based composite indices and machine learning for governance analytics:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.5em]

  \item \textbf{Two-level deterministic CRITIC weighting.} We develop and apply
    a two-tier CRITIC procedure to the \PAPI panel (2011--2024). Level~1
    derives within-criterion local weights for the 4--3 sub-criteria belonging
    to each of the 8 main criteria; Level~2 derives cross-criteria weights
    from a criterion-level composite matrix. Global sub-criterion weights are
    the product of the two levels, inheriting the simplex property.
    Temporal stability---operationalised as the split-half cosine similarity
    of weight vectors---reaches $0.9915$, substantially above the $0.95$
    threshold, confirming robustness of the weighting scheme across the
    14-year horizon.

  \item \textbf{Multi-method MCDM consensus via Evidential Reasoning.}
    We simultaneously apply six established \MCDM methods---\TOPSIS
    \citep{Hwang1981}, \VIKOR \citep{Opricovic2004}, PROMETHEE~II
    \citep{Brans1985}, \COPRAS \citep{Zavadskas1994}, \EDAS
    \citep{Keshavarz2015}, and \SAW \citep{Fishburn1967}---and aggregate
    their ordinal outputs through a two-stage Hierarchical Evidential
    Reasoning procedure \citep{Yang2002}. The resulting Kendall concordance
    coefficient $W \approx 0.88$ indicates very strong inter-method agreement,
    lending credibility to the final consensus ranking.

  \item \textbf{State-of-the-art ensemble learning for governance forecasting.}
    We construct a three-tier predictive system consisting of: (i)~five
    diverse base models---CatBoost~\citep{Prokhorenkova2018},
    Bayesian Ridge, Quantile Random Forest, Kernel Ridge Regression, and
    Support Vector Regression---trained on a
    12-block temporal feature engineering pipeline generating $\approx 295$
    panel features per province; (ii)~a Super Learner meta-ensemble
    \citep{vanderLaan2007} combining base models via Non-Negative Least
    Squares (NNLS) with panel-aware walk-forward cross-validation; and
    (iii)~distribution-free $95\%$ conformal prediction intervals
    \citep{Angelopoulos2022} calibrated from out-of-fold residuals, providing
    statistically rigorous uncertainty quantification for 2025 forecasts.

  \item \textbf{Integrated MCDM--forecasting pipeline.}
    By applying the validated weighting and consensus ranking procedure to the
    machine-learning-generated 2025 provincial scores, we produce the first
    forward-looking \PAPI governance ranking, enabling decision-makers to
    anticipate provincial performance trajectories one year in advance. This
    integration constitutes a reusable, open-source analytical framework for
    annual update as new survey waves become available.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Scope and data}

The empirical analysis covers all 63 Vietnamese provinces and centrally
administered municipalities observed annually from 2011 to 2024 (14 complete
survey waves). The raw dataset comprises 882 province-year observations across
29 sub-criteria, yielding a $882 \times 29$ balanced panel with \SI{13.4}{\percent}
structural missingness that is handled through a combination of temporal
back-filling, column-median imputation, and---for the forecasting stage---a
machine-learning-based panel imputation procedure. The target year for
prospective forecasting is 2025, defined as the first year outside the
observed sample.

\subsection*{Paper organisation}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the data and presents the full three-pillar
methodology: two-level CRITIC weighting (Section~\ref{subsec:critic}), the six
\MCDM methods and their Evidential Reasoning fusion
(Sections~\ref{subsec:mcdm}--\ref{subsec:er}), and the ensemble forecasting
pipeline with conformal prediction
(Sections~\ref{subsec:features}--\ref{subsec:conformal}).
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports empirical results, covering weighting
diagnostics, historical and forecast rankings, model performance, and
prediction intervals. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the findings,
discusses policy implications, and benchmarks our methodology against
alternative approaches in the literature. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
concludes with a summary of contributions, limitations, and directions for
future research.
